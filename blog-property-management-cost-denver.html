How much does property management cost in Denver?

A straight-talking, 2026 breakdown of what management really costs across the Denver metro — and how to tell when you're getting your money's worth.

By Nadir Maaiah·July 8, 2026·6 min read

The short answer

  • Most Denver-metro managers charge 8%–12% of monthly rent — about $160–$240 on a $2,000 rental.
  • Expect a one-time leasing fee (50%–100% of a month's rent) when a new tenant is placed, and a smaller renewal fee.
  • The cheapest fee is rarely the best deal — one long vacancy or one bad tenant costs far more than a year of management.
  • Management fees are tax-deductible, which softens the real cost.

It's the first question almost every owner asks us: what's this going to cost me? It's a fair one — and most company websites dodge it. So here's a transparent look at what property management actually costs in Denver, Arvada, Westminster, and the surrounding metro in 2026, what each fee pays for, and how to judge whether a manager is worth it.

The monthly management fee

The core cost is the ongoing management fee, and across the Denver metro it typically lands at 8% to 12% of the monthly rent you collect. On a home renting for $2,000 a month, that's roughly $160 to $240. Some companies charge a flat monthly fee instead — usually $100 to $500 depending on the property — which can favor higher-rent homes.

A percentage model quietly aligns your manager's incentives with yours: they only earn when the unit is rented and the rent is actually collected, so keeping your property occupied and cash flowing is in everyone's interest.

The leasing (tenant-placement) fee

When a new tenant is placed, most managers charge a one-time leasing fee of 50% to 100% of one month's rent, or a flat $500 to $1,000. This is not a hidden upcharge — it pays for the most labor-intensive part of the whole relationship: professional photos and marketing, fielding inquiries, showings, thorough applicant screening, and drafting a lease that holds up under Colorado law. Because it's a placement fee, you only pay it when a qualified resident actually signs.

Lease renewal fees

When a good tenant renews, you'll often see a smaller renewal fee of $100 to $300 (or 25%–50% of a month's rent). It covers re-papering the lease and any renegotiation. It should always be a fraction of a new-placement fee — if a company charges the same to renew as to place, that's a flag.

The fees to actually watch for

The sticker percentage isn't where owners get surprised. It's the add-ons. Before you sign, ask any company to put these in writing:

  • Vacancy fees — some charge you even when no one is paying rent. We don't believe you should pay to manage an empty house.
  • Maintenance markups — a percentage added on top of every contractor invoice.
  • Marketing / setup / onboarding fees charged before a tenant is even found.
  • Long lock-in contracts with steep cancellation penalties.

Is a property manager worth the cost?

For most owners, yes — and the math is usually not close. A vacant month on a $2,000 rental is $2,000 gone, which alone exceeds a full year of the difference between a "cheap" and a quality manager. One eviction of a poorly screened tenant can cost thousands more. A good manager earns their fee by filling vacancies faster, screening out the tenants who cause those losses, and keeping you compliant with Colorado's tightening landlord-tenant rules.

Two things soften the cost further: the management fee is tax-deductible as an ordinary business expense, and a professionally managed, well-maintained home tends to command stronger rent and longer tenancies. If you're weighing it, our guide on whether a property manager is worth it walks through the full trade-off.

What you should get for the fee

Cost only means something next to what's included. A full-service Denver-metro manager should cover marketing and leasing, tenant screening, rent collection, 24/7 maintenance coordination, inspections, monthly owner statements, and legal compliance. If a low headline rate leaves half of that as "extra," it isn't really cheaper.

Frequently asked questions

What percentage do property managers charge in Denver?

Most charge 8%–12% of the monthly rent collected — about $160–$240 on a $2,000 rental. Some offer a flat fee of $100–$500 per month instead.

Do I pay a fee when the property is vacant?

With a percentage-based manager, generally no — if no rent comes in, no management fee is charged. Watch for companies that bill a flat vacancy fee.

Are property management fees tax-deductible?

Yes. The IRS treats management fees as an ordinary, necessary rental business expense, so they're deductible against your rental income. See our rental property tax deductions guide.
