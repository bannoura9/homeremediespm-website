Is a property manager worth it?

Every landlord asks this at some point — usually after a 2 a.m. maintenance call or a tenant who just stopped paying. Here's an honest, numbers-based answer for owners deciding between self-managing and hiring help in the Denver metro.

By Nadir Maaiah·June 24, 2026·6 min read

The short answer

  • Self-managing isn't free just because there's no line-item fee — it's effectively a part-time job: 24/7 maintenance calls, chasing rent, showings, and legal compliance.
  • Management fees typically run 8%–12% of monthly rent (or a flat $100–$500/month) and are tax-deductible.
  • The real cost of self-managing is usually one long vacancy or one bad tenant — either can cost more than a full year of management fees.
  • If you own multiple units, live far away, don't know Colorado landlord-tenant law, or a vacancy keeps dragging past 30 days, it's time to hire.

"Can't I just manage it myself?" Sure — plenty of owners do, especially with one property close to home. But the question isn't really whether you can self-manage. It's whether the time, risk, and hassle you're taking on is actually worth what you'd save. Let's walk through the real numbers on both sides.

What self-managing really costs: your time

There's no fee on your statement for self-managing, but that doesn't mean it's free. In practice, it's a part-time job. You're the one who answers the maintenance call at 11 p.m. when a pipe bursts, the one who has to track down rent that's three days late, the one running showings around your own work schedule, and the one expected to know current legal compliance requirements without a law degree. None of that shows up as a dollar figure — but it's real time you're spending, and it doesn't pause when you're on vacation or dealing with your own family emergencies.

Add it up over a year and it's dozens of hours: coordinating a plumber on short notice, screening applicants, drafting or updating a lease, and being the person a tenant calls when something goes wrong on a Sunday. Some owners genuinely enjoy that work. Others realize a few months in that they signed up for an investment, not a second job.

The budget reality of owning a rental

Whether you self-manage or hire help, a rental has costs beyond the mortgage. Plan on annual non-mortgage costs — maintenance, turnover, insurance, vacancy — running roughly 35% to 45% of your yearly rental income, and budget for one to two vacant months a year. That's just the baseline reality of owning rental property in Colorado. The real question is who absorbs that risk and how well it's managed: a property sitting vacant for 45 days because nobody's actively marketing it is a very different outcome than a 15-day turnaround.

Signs it's time to hire a property manager

Self-managing works fine for some owners. It stops working for others, and there are usually clear signs. It's probably time to hire when:

  • You own multiple units and can't give each one enough attention.
  • You live far from the property — out of state or even across the metro.
  • You genuinely don't have the time for maintenance calls, showings, and rent follow-up.
  • You're not confident you know Colorado landlord-tenant law — see our rundown of common first-time landlord mistakes and what happens when you fall behind on the Colorado eviction process.
  • You're stuck with a difficult tenant and don't know your options.
  • Your vacancy has been sitting longer than 30 days with no clear plan to fill it.

What a good manager actually delivers

Here's how to judge whether a manager is doing their job, in plain numbers. A healthy manager should fill a vacancy in roughly 15 to 30 days — if it's dragging past 30 days, that's a red flag, not a normal wait. On renewals, a strong manager keeps roughly 75% of tenants renewing, well above the industry-average turnover rate of around 50%. Every turnover costs money — cleaning, marketing, screening, and lost rent during the gap — so a higher renewal rate compounds in your favor year over year. A good manager also keeps you compliant with Colorado's tightening rules around security deposits, just-cause eviction, and Fair Housing, which matters more every year. Does it pencil out? Fees vs. the cost of doing it yourself

Management fees in the Denver metro typically run 8% to 12% of monthly rent, or a flat $100–$500 a month for some companies — we break down the full fee structure, including leasing and renewal fees, in our Denver property management cost guide. Those fees are also tax-deductible as an ordinary rental business expense, which softens the real cost further. Now compare that to the downside of self-managing: one long vacancy or one bad tenant who ends up in eviction can easily exceed a full year of management fees — in lost rent, legal costs, and the time it takes to clean up the mess. The fee isn't really what you're buying. You're buying protection against the expensive mistakes that happen when a property sits unmanaged.

Run the comparison on your own property: take a year of management fees at 8%–12% of rent, and stack it against what a single 60-day vacancy or one contested eviction would cost you in lost rent and legal fees. For most owners, that comparison is what actually settles the question — not the sticker percentage on its own.

When self-managing still makes sense

None of this means every owner needs a manager. If you have one property close to home, genuinely enjoy landlord work, have the time to be reachable for maintenance and showings, and are comfortable with Colorado's landlord-tenant law, self-managing can work well. The math above is here so you can make that call with real numbers instead of a guess — and revisit it if your situation changes, whether that's adding a second property or moving out of state.

Frequently asked questions

Is hiring a property manager worth the cost in Denver?

For most owners, yes. Management fees typically run 8%–12% of monthly rent and are tax-deductible, while the real cost of self-managing is usually one long vacancy or one bad tenant — either can exceed a full year of management fees. A good manager also fills vacancies faster and keeps you compliant with Colorado law.

How do I know it's time to hire a property manager?

It's time when you own multiple units, live far from the property, don't have time for 24/7 maintenance calls and showings, aren't confident on Colorado landlord-tenant law, are stuck with a difficult tenant, or a vacancy keeps dragging past 30 days.

Can I deduct property management fees on my taxes?

Yes. The IRS treats property management fees as an ordinary, necessary rental business expense, so they're deductible against your rental income, which softens the real cost of hiring a manager.
